Motivation

  • Distributional semantics and neural language models have been a dominant approach in language representation models for a variety of NLP tasks for nearly a decade.
  • Primarily optimised for capturing statistical co-occurrence relations at scale, which can be later translated as semantic relatedness and semantic similarity measures for any given pair of terms in a corpus.
  • Understanding intrinsic linguistic/semantic properties of distributional/neural language models can provide important insight on their capabilities and limitations beyond semantic relatedness/similarity, enabling some degree of formal verification, and their use in applications with higher safety/criticality requirements.

Motivation

  • We focus on compositional properties, i.e., how the meaning of two or more terms combine in a phrase to build the meaning of the utterance.
  • Sentence embeddings are the primary target for analysis, as they intrinsically perform some kind of composition, independently on the task they were trained for.
  • Two scenarios:
    • Measuring consistency towards modifier phenomena, specifically on adjective phrases (ANs).
    • Examining latent space characteristics on autoencoded definition/explanatory sentences with SRL induced biases.

Modifier phenomena in NL

  • Modification: a set of compositional principles regarding intensional interpretations from a Montagovian formalism (denotations).
  • Adjective phrases being the object of analysis
  • Adjective types:
    • Intersective (or extensional): describe the intersection of the noun denotation with one from the adjective itself.
      Example: “red car” denotes the set of things that are both “car” and “red”. Lambda abstraction: (λx.[car(x) ∧ red(x)]).
    • Subsective (non-intersective): describe a strict subset of the noun denotation it modifies.
      Example: “skillful teacher” denotes a subset of teachers, but there is no denotation for “skillful”: (λx.[skillful(teacher(x))]).
    • Privative non-subsective: describe a set that is completely disjoint from the denotation of the noun it modifies. Example: “fake wall” denotes the set of things that are definitely not walls: (λx.[fake(wall(x))]).
    • Plain non-subsective: describe a set that may or may not be a subset of the modified noun's denotation, depending on the adjective itself or the context.
      Examples: “alleged criminal” denotes a set for which inclusion to the denotation of “criminal” is dubious or undefined: (λx.[alleged(criminal(x))]). “former president” denotes a set of individuals that were president in a preceding term: (λx.[f ormer(president(x))]).
    • Ambiguous: can be applied as any of the previous categories, depending on the noun it modifies and the context.
      Example: in “big truck” the interpretation of “big” is intersective, while in “big fool” is subsective non-intersective.

Montague denotations

We say that a noun $a$ can be modified by an adjective $b$ to form an adjective phrase $p = ba$.
For example: in the phrase "Canadian writer", we have the following Montague denotations (intensions):

$W(x) = [writer] = \lambda x.[writer(x)]$
$C(x) = [Canadian] = \lambda x.[Canadian(x)]$
$P(x) = [Canadian~writer] = \lambda x.[C(x) \land W(x)]$


and corresponding sets (extensions):

$P\equiv\{x~|~P(x)=\top\}, \quad W\equiv\{x~|~W(x)=\top\}, \quad C\equiv\{x~|~C(x)=\top\}$
$P = (C \cap W) \subset W$
$P = (C \cap W) \subset C$

Denotation set distance

The fact that $P$ is a subset of both $W$ and $C$ suggests the following distance relations between sets:

$d(P,W) \leq d(W, C)$
$d(P, C) \leq d(W, C)$


where $d$ is the Jaccard distance.

For longer phrases $p=b_1\dots b_na$ with $n$ adjectives, the distance relations can be generalised to:

$d(P, T_i) \leq d(T_j, T_k) \quad~\quad \forall{i, j, k};~~ j < k $

where $T_{1\dots n}$ are the sets of adjectives $b_{1\dots n}$.

Non-intersective denotations

Non-intersective adjectives are associated to different Montague denotations.
For example, the phrase $p=skilled\,writer$ yields the intensions:

$\phi(x) = \lambda x.[skilled(x)]$
$P(x) = \lambda x.[\phi(W(x))]$


and corresponding set (extension):

$P \equiv \{x~|~P(x)=\top\} \subset W$


where $\phi(x)$ does not have a corresponding set.

Illustrated example

On distributional neural models

  • We may expect models to preserve linguistic properties, in particular the ones dealing with compositionality (e.g., sentence embeddings).
  • Question: could such representations be employed as proxies for their denotations?
  • Conversely, could set theoretical denotation properties be interpreted in the embedding spaces in terms of geometrical properties?
  • This would allow term comparisons made in the embedding spaces to be interpreted as analogous to their denotations (e.g., quantifying subset inclusion), vice-versa.

Embedding-denotation analogy

  • Hypothesis: We expect a large language model to be empirically consistent with the formal properties of the modifier phenomena.
  • In other words, we expect the distance between embeddings in the latent space to be analogous to the set distance between denotations.
  • We can then measure consistency w.r.t. metamorphic relations over the distance relations, due to the stochastic nature of the language models.

Embedding-denotation analogy -- Tests

Consistency tests

  • Testing intersectivity (single phrase):
    • $I_{m,p} \equiv d(emb_m(p), emb_m(t_i)) \leq d(emb_m(t_j), emb_m(t_k)) \qquad\forall{i, j, k};~~ j < k$
    • $E_{m, L}\big\{I_{m,p} = \top\big\}, \quad p \sim L$
  • Testing intersectivity (phrase pairs):
    • $II_{m,\{p\}} = d(emb_m(p_{b_1a_1}), emb_m(p_{b_1a_2})) \leq d(emb_m(p_{b_2a_1}), emb_m(p_{b_2a_2}))$
    • $E_{m, L^2}\big\{II_{m,\{p\}} = \top\big\}, \quad \{p\} \sim L^2$
  • Testing non-subsectivity:
    • $NI_{m,p} = d(emb_m(p), emb_m(b)) \leq d(emb_m(p), emb_m(a))$
    • $E_{m, L}\big\{NI_{m,p} = \top\big\}, \quad p \sim L$

Results [1 and 2 adjectives]

  • Single adjective results indicate that all of the considered models tend to satisfy the intersectivity property. Thus treating compositions intersectively.
  • We start seeing starker differences once we consider composed pairs of adjectives.
  • Adjective pairs where neither of the types are intersective yield the lowest scores, especially when the type is repeated.

Results [phrase pairs]

  • The comparison between phrase pairs in which only the noun changes in each pair indicates there is an intersectivity hierarchy for each model.
  • BERT, for example, would be: S–I > S–N I > N S–P r > N S–P l > A.
  • Optimizations of BERT architecture (DistilRoBERTa, MiniLM, T5) show a more pronounced intersective effect than base BERT, which may indicate a relation of the degree of information compression to the intersective effect.

Results [non-intersectivity]

  • For the non-intersectivity results, behaviour is as expected from BERT: intersective adjectives display a much higher consistency for the tested property, while non-subsective ones are all much lower.
  • While adjective type differences display relatively low compositional effects on broad intersectivity in the evaluated models, the effects are noticeable in single adjective interactions, indicating such differences are encoded in individual word representations, but they do not transfer generally in the expected way to the compositions.
  • Unexpectedly, this also applies to subsective non-intersective adjectives to the same or higher extent, even in the non-contextual models.
  • DistilRoberta and T5 differ from BERT in their treatment of privative non-subsective adjectives, perhaps indicating that this phenomenon is not as strongly modelled in the hypothesized way for these models.

In conclusion

  • Our results indicate that current neural language models do not behave consistently according to expected behavior from the formalisms, with regard to the evaluated intersective property
  • Whether this is a limitation of current models or of the linguistic theoretical framework is still an open question.
  • On the other hand, the results also show that conforming adjective type differences are encoded by the LMs at word level, which motivates further examination on compositional capabilities of state-of-the art LMs.

Achieving control: SRL induced disentanglement

  • When trying to improve language representation models for inference tasks, one may be pointed towards the structural connection between the elements of an inference chain.
  • Typically, an abstraction or realization process will happen to the subject or object of a sentence, leading to the next step in the chain.
  • Inducing structural biases to sentence encodings could lead to NLI improvements, but we first need to understand the latent space characteristics, and how to leverage them.

Achieving control: SRL induced disentanglement

  • We thus started investigating sentence embeddings obtained by leveraging semantic structures at sentence level for disentanglement.
  • One possible way of connecting the disentanglement of latent representations to changes in task performance is by characterizing them in terms of geometrical properties.
  • We developed a methodology to assess said properties in terms of consistency on vector operations in a contextual latent space (and in a dual manner, enforcing geometrical consistency within this space).

Latent space hypotheses

  • Given the architectural constraints imposed and the supervision involved, we elaborated a set of hypotheses regarding how the information might be encoded in the latent space
  • From those, we can derive a geometric characterization that can be possibly exploited by downstream models.
  • Hypotheses:
    • 1. Semantically separable: only sentence content (lexical semantics) information is encoded. Semantic role conditioning is not encoded in the model, but steer the content into more clearly separated clusters, thus improving disentanglement.
    • 2. Jointly structurally-semantically separable: both content and role (structural) information are encoded. Each combination role-content is described by a hypersolid in the latent space, thus improving disentanglement.
    • 3. Structurally consistent: a given vector operation will likely preserve at least one role or change it in the same way for any pair of vectors, independent on the content.
    • 4. Semantically consistent: a given vector operation will likely preserve the meaning (or topic) of the sentence or change it in the same way for any pair of vectors, independent on the topic.

Testing hypotheses

  • We test the hypotheses by employing a methodology comprising of:
    1. An algorithm for calculating statistical separability & consistency.
    2. An algorithm for estimating argument-role pair separability.
    3. Qualitative evaluation of latent space traversals and interpolation

Summary of results

  • Hypotheses 1 - 4 were successfuly validated to different degrees.
  • Content-role pairs are encoded in hypersolids ("regions" or "clusters"), allowing limited level of control.
  • Vector sum operation is very consistent across regions.
  • Content frequency in the corpus defines region size.
  • Overlapping regions can be used for quantifying ambiguity.
