What is Representation Learning?

  • Learning representations of the data that make it easier to extract useful information when building classifiers or other predictor.
    (Bengio et al., 2014)
  • Replaces manual feature engineering.
  • Ideally captures the posterior distribution of the underlying explanatory factors for the observed input. (Bengio et al., 2014)

What makes a representation good?

  • A good representation is one that describes well the phenomena of interest for a particular use: space, time, hierarchy, among others.
    • For any given representation there are typically trade-offs regarding which phenomena are prioritised and which will have acceptable imprecision or distortions.
    • Common criteria include: smoothness, disentanglement, abstraction and linearity.

Smoothness

  • The notion that similar inputs should correspond to similar representations:
    $x \approx z \rightarrow f(x) \approx f(z)$

Abstraction

  • Finding common patterns or rule sets that describe a set of observations, allowing the omission of irrelevant details.

  • Probability mass concentrates near regions that have a much smaller dimensionality than the original space where the data lives:

Clustering

  • Different values of categorical variables such as object classes are associated with separate manifolds.
  • Local variations on the manifold tend to preserve the value of a category, and a linear interpolation between examples of different classes in general involves going through a low density region.
    (Bengio et al., 2014)

State-of-the-Art examples

Modifier phenomena in NL

  • Modification: a set of compositional principles regarding intensional interpretations from a Montagovian formalism (denotations).
  • Adjective phrases being the object of analysis
  • Adjective types:
    • Intersective (or extensional): describe the intersection of the noun denotation with one from the adjective itself.
    • Subsective (non-intersective): describe a strict subset of the noun denotation it modifies.
    • Privative non-subsective: describe a set that is completely disjoint from the denotation of the noun it modifies.
    • Plain non-subsective: describe a set that may or may not be a subset of the modified noun's denotation, depending on the adjective itself or the context.
    • Ambiguous: can be applied as any of the previous categories, depending on the noun it modifies and the context.
      Example: in “big truck” the interpretation of “big” is intersective, while in “big fool” is subsective non-intersective.

Montague denotations

We say that a noun $a$ can be modified by an adjective $b$ to form an adjective phrase $p = ba$.
For example: in the phrase "Canadian writer", we have the following Montague denotations (intensions):

$W(x) = [writer] = \lambda x.[writer(x)]$
$C(x) = [Canadian] = \lambda x.[Canadian(x)]$
$P(x) = [Canadian~writer] = \lambda x.[C(x) \land W(x)]$


and corresponding sets (extensions):

$P\equiv\{x~|~P(x)=\top\}, \quad W\equiv\{x~|~W(x)=\top\}, \quad C\equiv\{x~|~C(x)=\top\}$
$P = (C \cap W) \subset W$
$P = (C \cap W) \subset C$

Denotation set distance

The fact that $P$ is a subset of both $W$ and $C$ suggests the following distance relations between sets:

$d(P,W) \leq d(W, C)$
$d(P, C) \leq d(W, C)$


where $d$ is the Jaccard distance.

For longer phrases $p=b_1\dots b_na$ with $n$ adjectives, the distance relations can be generalised to:

$d(P, T_i) \leq d(T_j, T_k) \quad~\quad \forall{i, j, k};~~ j < k $

where $T_{1\dots n}$ are the sets of adjectives $b_{1\dots n}$.

Non-intersective denotations

Non-intersective adjectives are associated to different Montague denotations.
For example, the phrase $p=skilled\,writer$ yields the intensions:

$\phi(x) = \lambda x.[skilled(x)]$
$P(x) = \lambda x.[\phi(W(x))]$


and corresponding set (extension):

$P \equiv \{x~|~P(x)=\top\} \subset W$


where $\phi(x)$ does not have a corresponding set.

On distributional neural models

  • We may expect models to preserve linguistic properties, in particular the ones dealing with compositionality (e.g., sentence embeddings).
  • Question: could such representations be employed as proxies for their denotations?
  • Conversely, could set theoretical denotation properties be interpreted in the embedding spaces in terms of geometrical properties?
  • This would allow term comparisons made in the embedding spaces to be interpreted as analogous to their denotations (e.g., quantifying subset inclusion), vice-versa.

Consistency tests

  • Testing intersectivity (single phrase):
    • $I_{m,p} \equiv d(emb_m(p), emb_m(t_i)) \leq d(emb_m(t_j), emb_m(t_k)) \qquad\forall{i, j, k};~~ j < k$
    • $E_{m, L}\big\{I_{m,p} = \top\big\}, \quad p \sim L$
  • Testing intersectivity (phrase pairs):
    • $II_{m,\{p\}} = d(emb_m(p_{b_1a_1}), emb_m(p_{b_1a_2})) \leq d(emb_m(p_{b_2a_1}), emb_m(p_{b_2a_2}))$
    • $E_{m, L^2}\big\{II_{m,\{p\}} = \top\big\}, \quad \{p\} \sim L^2$
  • Testing non-subsectivity:
    • $NI_{m,p} = d(emb_m(p), emb_m(b)) \leq d(emb_m(p), emb_m(a))$
    • $E_{m, L}\big\{NI_{m,p} = \top\big\}, \quad p \sim L$

Results [1 and 2 adjectives]

  • Single adjective results indicate that all of the considered models tend to satisfy the intersectivity property. Thus treating compositions intersectively.

Results [phrase pairs]

  • The comparison between phrase pairs in which only the noun changes in each pair indicates there is an intersectivity hierarchy for each model.

Results [non-intersectivity]

  • Intersective adjectives display a much higher consistency for the tested property (BERT), while non-subsective ones are all much lower.
  • Adjective type differences are encoded in individual word representations, but they do not transfer generally in the expected way to the compositions.

In conclusion

  • Our results indicate that current neural language models do not behave consistently according to expected behavior from the formalisms, with regard to the evaluated intersective property
  • Whether this is a limitation of current models or of the linguistic theoretical framework is still an open question.
  • On the other hand, the results also show that conforming adjective type differences are encoded by the LMs at word level, which motivates further examination on compositional capabilities of state-of-the art LMs.
